## LinkedIn (primary)

I have called myself a translator for years.

Not as a slogan. As a job description.

I have been the player receiving information. I have trained young athletes trying to understand their development. I have worked with NBA teams on player-tendency and analytics questions. I have advised the father of an NBA player on how to interpret basketball analytics and connect them to development.

The tools keep changing. The gap does not.

Sports organizations do not lack data, film, or dashboards. They lack consistent translation: the work of turning a true finding into language each audience can use.

An analyst may need confidence in the measurement.  
A coach may need a few relevant possessions.  
A player may need one clear cue and a chance to test it.

That is not watering analytics down. It is completing the work.

College and pro staffs also work under real constraints—time, attention, and the limits of working memory. More clips are not automatically better teaching. Focused film and one actionable instruction often beat a highlight dump that leaves everyone nodding and nobody changing.

This is why Hoop Tendency Experts matter: people who can move between the court, the film room, and the spreadsheet without losing either the truth of the number or the feel of the game. At the end of the day, analytics are tendencies. Analysts protect the measurement. Hoop Tendency Experts help the organization use it. The strongest staffs need both.

I wrote a longer piece on Sportstechwest about the translation gap, pattern recognition, and how BallCODE and BallCODEChess extend the same principle into education and daily mental reps.

Read it here:  
https://sportstechwest.com/blog/i-have-called-myself-a-translator-for-years/

The future will not belong only to the organizations with the most data. It will belong to the ones that can turn data into understanding—and understanding into action.

— Rashad West | Sportstechwest

Focused observation  
A small number of relevant clips  
One clear cue
