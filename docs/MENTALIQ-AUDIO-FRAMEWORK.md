# mentalIQ — Mental Focus for Sports (Audio Framework)

**Format:** Audio only  
**Structure:** Segments with **5 seconds silence** between each  
**Two versions:** 3-minute and 1-hour

---

## Framework Rules

| Rule | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Silence between segments** | 5 seconds |
| **3-minute version** | 8 segments = 8 hour-parts compressed; 7 × 5 sec gaps (~2 min 25 sec speech + gaps ≈ 3 min) |
| **1-hour version** | ~60 segments (content ~55 min, gaps ~5 min) |
| **Segments** | One idea per segment. Pause 5 sec after each. |

## mentalIQ — 1-Hour Script (Audio)

**Total segments:** 60  
**Structure:** 60 segments with 5 seconds between each  
**Approx. content:** ~55 min  
**5-second gaps:** 59 × 5 sec ≈ 5 min  
**Total:** ~60 min

---

### Part 1: Introduction — Segments 1–6

**Segment 1**  
Mental focus in sports isn’t magic. It’s a skill. This is mentalIQ — a one-hour framework for building and using mental focus in training and competition.

**Segment 2**  
We’ll cover what focus is, why it breaks down under pressure, and three practical systems you can start using today.

**Segment 3**  
By the end, you’ll have a simple plan you can apply this week. Let’s start.

**Segment 4**  
Focus is attention on the right thing at the right time. In sport, that usually means: this play, this moment, this decision.

**Segment 5**  
There are two directions your attention can go. Internal: thoughts, emotions, self-talk. External: the ball, the opponent, the field.

**Segment 6**  
Elite performers switch between them deliberately. Before a play: internal. During the play: external. After: brief internal check, then back to external.

---

### Part 2: Why Focus Breaks Down — Segments 7–12

**Segment 7**  
The problem isn’t lack of focus. It’s focus on the wrong thing.

**Segment 8**  
Under pressure, three things usually happen. One: narrowing. You fixate on one thing and miss the bigger picture.

**Segment 9**  
Two: internal takeover. Your attention moves inward. Don’t miss. What if I mess up. I’m tired. That’s focus on threat, not task.

**Segment 10**  
Three: rushing or freezing. Your system is overloading. Instead of a clear process, you react too fast or get stuck.

**Segment 11**  
The fix isn’t try harder. It’s having a process that works under stress.

**Segment 12**  
That’s what we build next: three systems.

---

### Part 3: System 1 — Breathing — Segments 13–20

**Segment 13**  
System one: breathing. Breathing is the fastest way to change your state.

**Segment 14**  
We’ll use box breathing. In for four, hold for four, out for four, hold for four. One cycle is about sixteen seconds.

**Segment 15**  
When to use it: five to ten minutes before competition.

**Segment 16**  
In the huddle or during a stoppage.

**Segment 17**  
Right after a mistake, before the next play.

**Segment 18**  
Why it works: it activates the parasympathetic nervous system. It gives your mind one clear task.

**Segment 19**  
Practice now. Pause and do two full cycles. In for four, hold for four, out for four, hold for four.

**Segment 20**  
Notice how your body feels. That’s the reset you can use in games.

---

### Part 4: System 2 — Cue Words — Segments 21–28

**Segment 21**  
System two: cue words. A cue word brings you back to your process when your mind wanders.

**Segment 22**  
Examples: Process. Lock in. Next play. Smooth. Here.

**Segment 23**  
When to use it: right before you execute. Free throw, serve, kick.

**Segment 24**  
After a distraction or error. When you feel yourself overthinking.

**Segment 25**  
How it works: trigger — you notice you’re distracted. Cue — you say your word. Action — you do the next thing.

**Segment 26**  
The word becomes an anchor.

**Segment 27**  
Practice now. Choose one cue word. Say it out loud three times.

**Segment 28**  
Use it before your next practice rep.

---

### Part 5: System 3 — Routines — Segments 29–36

**Segment 29**  
System three: routines. A routine is a short sequence you do the same way every time before execution.

**Segment 30**  
Bounce the ball twice, deep breath, look at the rim, shoot. Or: step behind the ball, one practice swing, breathe, putt.

**Segment 31**  
Why it works: the routine removes decisions. You’re not choosing what to do; you’re following steps.

**Segment 32**  
That frees attention for the actual skill.

**Segment 33**  
When to use it: free throws, penalties, serves, putts, pre-pitch. Any time you have a few seconds.

**Segment 34**  
Practice now. Write down a five- to ten-second routine for one key skill.

**Segment 35**  
Rehearse it mentally, then use it in your next practice.

**Segment 36**  
Build the habit.

---

### Part 6: Putting It Together — Segments 37–42

**Segment 37**  
Pre-competition: five to ten minutes of breath work. Get your system calm.

**Segment 38**  
During competition: use your cue word when you feel drift or tension.

**Segment 39**  
Use your routine before every key execution.

**Segment 40**  
After a mistake: one breath. Say your cue. Back to the next play.

**Segment 41**  
You don’t need to do everything at once. Start with breathing. Add a cue word. Build a routine.

**Segment 42**  
One piece at a time.

---

### Part 7: Common Mistakes — Segments 43–48

**Segment 43**  
Mistake one: only using it in games. Practice your focus skills in low-stakes situations first.

**Segment 44**  
Mistake two: changing routines or cues too often. Consistency matters. Pick something and stick with it for weeks.

**Segment 45**  
Mistake three: overdoing it. One or two breaths. One cue word. One short routine. Keep it simple.

---

### Part 8: Closing — Segments 49–60

**Segment 49**  
Your body can only do what your mind lets it.

**Segment 50**  
mentalIQ is about training your mind the way you train your body.

**Segment 51**  
This week: do two or three box breaths before every practice.

**Segment 52**  
Choose one cue word and use it before key reps.

**Segment 53**  
Write and use one short routine for one skill.

**Segment 54**  
Build the habit. Trust the process.

**Segments 55–60**  
[Space for recap, reflection prompts, or extended silence for practice. Each segment: 5 sec gap after.]

**Segment 60**  
That’s mentalIQ. More next time.

- **Audio:** Voice only; no B-roll or visual cues.
- **3-min:** ~8 segments. Use for shorts, social, quick intro.
- **1-hour:** ~60 segments. Use for deep session, workshop, guided practice.
